## Introduction

Have you ever wondered how to make a computer program, or even just how a program works, but never really started coding?

That is a question I asked myself when I was nine years old. At the time, I was playing Minecraft with a mod called "ComputerCraft". The mod let me write programs to automate parts of the game, such as farming, mining, or simply opening doors with a password.

I took it as a game, and it was very fun. Every program was written in the Lua programming language, a small scripting language that felt approachable in the same way Python often does.

That experience alone led me to learn more programming languages, frameworks, and tools, such as C#, C++, Windows Forms, Win32 native Windows applications, DirectX 9, Unity, Godot, and web development.

### Coding and Education

Generally, in school, you learn the basic principles of coding, but often without a personal purpose. That depends on the teacher, of course, but most of the time it doesn't look fun. Very few people find joy in generating Fibonacci numbers in C++ just because someone told them to.

Writing code with a purpose, and doing it for fun, made my passion for coding stronger. I still think it is the best way to start.

I found myself more committed to studying the subject because I had reasons to use what I learned. Programming became a set of tools that I could use to build almost anything I imagined.

## What Is the Secret to Start Coding?

Quite a lot of people have told me that they don't know where to start. Many fear that they need to find someone, or some institution, to teach them coding. But I think it is much simpler than that.

The secret is to have fun by making silly projects and learning through practice.

Think of it as a playground. Imagine you're a child with a set of toys, but nobody tells you how to play with them. Fueled by curiosity and an exploring spirit, you learn more about the toys and how they work. After that, creativity and imagination come into play, and you find yourself creating your own stories.

> Curiosity is a very powerful thing in our human nature, why not use it to our advantage?

### Does Coding Require Passion?

I think it does, but to understand if you like it, you need to try it first, right?

Not everyone falls in love with it immediately, and that's fine. I happened to know very quickly that coding was my thing, but the more interesting question is what yours could be. The only way to find out is to start making something, anything, and pay attention to how it feels when it works.

### What About the Theory?

In the beginning, theory comes second. As soon as you start, you're going to find yourself researching how to do X and Y. But that is exactly what programming is also about: a lot of research is involved.

Then you will reach a point where you know how to do more things on your own, and the time you spend researching decreases a lot.

School and education certainly help by teaching some of the basic theory concepts of software development, like OOP (Object Oriented Programming) and algorithms. But it's still up to you to decide how to use that information, and you can learn a lot more by working on something than by only studying it.

### Some Examples of Programs I Made While Learning

Let me give you an example of a silly program I made when I was learning how to program. It was called "Spammer Prime", and its purpose was to flood WhatsApp group chats and make my friends' phones crash.

Obviously, this was not a noble project. It was childish, chaotic, and probably annoying to everyone around me. But it was a real project, and that mattered: I had a goal, I hit obstacles, and I learned how to make the computer do something I imagined.

It could also be used on Minecraft or basically anything else, because it emulated a keyboard. It would write text, press Enter, and repeat. I also set up shortcuts, made a cool UI, and had lots of fun making it.

Another example is "Easy Homework", which was basically a calculator that wrote down the entire process of converting numbers from one base to another, like from binary to decimal or from decimal to hex. I used that program to copy and paste my homework for school.

Again: not exactly heroic. But it was useful to me at the time, and building it taught me more than a clean textbook exercise would have.

> These are just some of the silliest ones that I made, but with your own imagination, you could make even cooler ones!

## Conclusion

Coding is a form of art. You have a set of tools and a blank page, and what you build is entirely up to you. The best projects won't come from a teacher's assignment. They'll come from something you actually wanted to make, however silly it seemed at the time.

That's what got me here. A kid with a mod, no plan, and too much free time.

You don't need a better starting point than that. You just need a reason to start, however small or silly it seems. The rest has a way of figuring itself out.

> If this resonated, share it with someone who keeps saying they'll start coding "someday."
