## Overview

This website was built as a place to present projects, describe my background in a way that goes beyond a CV, and create space for writing as well as development work.

Compared to some of my earlier portfolio projects, this one required a more deliberate balance between design, content, and personality. It was not only about publishing information, but about deciding how I wanted to present my work and how much of my voice the website should carry.

If I was going to ask people to trust my taste and hire me, the site had to do more than politely repeat the contents of my LinkedIn profile.

## Design Process

One of the main challenges was density. I had already written a significant amount of copy, which meant the design had to accommodate a lot of content without feeling heavy or static.

For the visual design process, I moved away from older Photoshop-based workflows and used **Penpot** instead. That made iteration much faster and helped me shape a more coherent system of components, typography, and layout decisions before development started.

That shift mattered more than it sounds. Photoshop gave me freedom, but not much speed. Penpot felt much closer to the way websites actually behave, which made the design process less of a wrestling match.

## Key Decisions

I wanted the website to feel crafted rather than assembled from familiar portfolio patterns. That meant building custom visual effects from scratch: hand-made SASS particle elements, and a soft animated "heartbeat" pulsing around the main heading on the home page.

The guiding principle for the homepage was simple: impress first, explain later. Text content lives exclusively in the about page, keeping the home screen intentionally minimal. A clean layout with a single call to action guides the reader straight there, where they can get a fuller sense of who I am and what I do.

On the about page, I used custom components, cards, icons, and grouped sections to break the information into a clearer visual structure, making it easy to scan.

::DcpImg{src="/images/blog/davide_portfolio_cards.png" alt="Skill Cards that you can flip"}
::

The desktop and mobile layouts also diverge deliberately in places where the content called for a different presentation, rather than a strict one-to-one adaptation. Responsive design is at its best when it behaves like design, not just a shrink-wrapped version of the desktop presentation.

::DcpImg{src="/images/blog/davide_portfolio_aboutexplained.png" alt="Digital marketing section icons, Desktop vs Mobile"}
::

## Writing and Content

An important consideration I had to make in the beginning was deciding whether to include a blog. Over time, writing had become a more meaningful part of my work, especially after handling copywriting for multiple clients, so I settled on the idea of adding it to publish not only finished work projects but also ideas, guides and reflections.

::DcpImg{src="/images/blog/davide_portfolio_copy.png" alt="Original Copywriting Document"}
::

That choice shaped the structure of the whole website. It is not just a gallery of projects, but a personal publishing space where design, code, and communication are all contained in one place.

I used to see writing as the annoying extra step after the technical work was done. At some point that flipped, and now I think the ability to explain decisions is part of the work, not the packaging around it.

Feedback was also a useful part of the process. When I got stuck, showing the design to other people helped me remember that the end reader was not me.

## Result

The finished website works as both a portfolio and a creative framework that I can continue to evolve over time. It gives me room to present technical work, document processes, and experiment with ideas in a space that reflects how I actually think about work.
